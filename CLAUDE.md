# Sanjay Sharma — Website Content Guide

## Who is this site for?
This is Sanjay Sharma's personal website. Its audience is a mix of:
- Engineering leaders and hiring managers in AI/ML
- Fellow engineers who found him via GitHub, LinkedIn, or a talk
- People curious about AI who may have seen his writing

---

## Voice & Persona

Sanjay is a **practitioner, not an academic**. He has built things that run in production at scale — not published papers about things that might work someday. His identity has three equal parts: he **builds** AI systems, he **teaches** AI to engineers, and he **learns** constantly. The tagline "I learn AI. I teach AI. I build AI." is the core.

Write as Sanjay in the **first person**. Direct. No hedging. No corporate fluff.

**Do:**
- Lead with what was built or what happened — not the title or the company name
- Use concrete specifics: numbers, names, outcomes ("45% latency reduction", "50+ engineers", "8 sessions")
- Show what you care about — the AI School wasn't mandated; he started it anyway
- Let curiosity and conviction show ("I care deeply about engineering culture")
- Write like someone who explains things clearly — because he teaches

**Don't:**
- Resumé-speak: "leveraged synergies", "drove alignment", "stakeholder engagement"
- Passive constructions: "was responsible for", "helped to", "was involved in"
- Vague claims without substance: "significant improvements", "major contributions"
- Overly formal hedging: "I'm seeking opportunities to..." (just describe what you do)
- Buzzword stacking without grounding it in a real system or outcome

---

## Bio vs Resume — the core distinction

This site should read as a **bio and capabilities showcase**, not a resume. The difference:

- **Resume**: "At Company X I built Y and achieved metric Z." — employer-centric, backward-looking, reads like a job application.
- **Bio/capabilities**: "Here's what I build, here's what I'm good at, here's what I care about." — person-centric, tells a story.

In practice this means:
- **About**: No company names. Talk about capabilities and character, not job history.
- **Work**: Narrative sentences per role, not bullet-point achievement lists. What was this era *about*? No bold metric callouts.
- **Projects**: Capability-descriptive titles (what type of system is this), not proprietary project codenames. No company attribution in descriptions.
- **Everywhere**: The reader should come away knowing *who you are and what you can do*, not just where you worked.

## Tone by Section

### Hero / Bio
Short. Punchy. Confident without being arrogant. Think: what would you say to someone at a conference who asked "what do you do?" One or two sentences that are actually interesting, not a job title.

> Bad: "Principal Engineer with 16+ years of experience in AI and ML specialising in enterprise platforms."
> Good: "I build AI systems that run in production. 16 years. 3 continents. One obsession."

### Work (Career Timeline)
Narrative-first. Start with what was *built or solved*, not the role. Use bullet points for specifics but open with a sentence that makes someone want to read further. Metrics are welcome — only when real.

### Projects
Each project should feel like a one-paragraph war story. What problem, what was non-obvious about the solution, what outcome. Technical details welcome; make them mean something.

### Writing / Talks
Title first, then context. No abstract filler. If the post/talk made a point, say what the point was.

### About
This is where personality is allowed to breathe. Write about what you care about and why — not just what you've done. The AI School story is gold: started it with no mandate because he believed the team deserved it. That's character.

## Content Principles

1. **Specific beats vague.** "Reduced deployment cycle time by 60%" > "improved deployment efficiency."
2. **Show the work.** Name the system, describe the architecture choice, give the constraint you were working under.
3. **Teaching is identity.** Any content opportunity that can connect to teaching or knowledge-sharing should — because it's genuinely who he is.
4. **Enterprise AI is real AI.** Don't undersell production at scale. Running LLM systems for a global asset manager is legitimately hard. Say so.
5. **Confidence without arrogance.** Karpathy writes "I was the Director of AI at Tesla" — no modesty softening. Do the same.
6. **Short sentences work.** A sentence fragment used well lands harder than a long compound clause.

---

## What to Avoid

- Do not turn bullet points into paragraph-length justifications. Trust the reader.
- Do not add qualifiers to strong statements ("sort of", "basically", "in a way").
- Do not use the phrase "thought leadership."
- Do not describe the AI School as a "programme" or "initiative" in the bio — it was a thing he *started*, describe it that way.
- Do not open any section with a generic adjective string: "Experienced, passionate, results-driven..."
